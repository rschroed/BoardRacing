# Product vision

## The fantasy

Board Racing is slot-car racing where one hand drives and the other runs the pit. The shared screen becomes the track, and physical Pieces make speed and strategy tangible.

## Audience and format

- 1–4 local players gathered around one Board.
- Family and social play rather than simulation enthusiasts alone.
- Races of approximately 6–10 minutes.
- Fast setup, clear recovery, and an easy rematch.

## Design pillars

### Tactile immediacy

The Pieces should feel like controls, not tokens placed to confirm an on-screen menu. Rotating, moving, placing, and holding them steady should produce immediate, legible feedback without relying on active touch recognition.

### Readable top-down action

Every player must be able to understand the race from any side of the table. Cars, track position, pit state, and hazards take priority over decorative detail.

### Meaningful pit decisions

The Pit Robot should create decisions that affect the race. It must not become a second throttle, a decorative status marker, or a sequence of arbitrary gestures.

### Quick rematchability

A finished race should naturally lead to another. Rules, setup, results, and restarts should minimize downtime.

## Core loop

1. Rotate the Driving Ship between Brake, Drive, and Boost while the digital car follows the track.
2. Manage corner speed, tire wear, and motor heat.
3. Decide when to call the pit with the Pit Robot.
4. Once parked, choose the service that best answers the live race conditions.
5. Perform the selected service with a short tactile interaction.
6. Return to the race and adapt the strategy.

## Initial scope boundaries

- No free-steering vehicle simulation.
- No online multiplayer in the first release.
- No combat or item system until the racing and pit loop work without it.
- No final art commitment before physical-control playtests.
- No large track library before one track produces repeatable fun.

## Open questions

- Are the provisional Brake, Drive, and Boost speeds and deceleration curves satisfying?
- Can players operate both Pieces without obscuring important action?
- How much precision can pit interactions require before they become frustrating?
- Is lane changing needed, and if so, which Piece should control it?
- Which information should be public, approximate, or revealed through the Pit Robot?
